ROMANS FOLKLORE

Roman folklore is the folklore of ancient Rome, including genres such as myth (Roman mythology), legend, joke, charms, fable, ghostlore, and numerous others.[1] Scholars have published a variety of collections focused on the folklore of ancient Rome.[2] Roman folklore is closely related to Ancient Greek folklore and precedes Italian folklore.
  • Founding legends of Romulus and Remus

    The tale of Romulus and Remus is perhaps the most well-known story. These twins are central figures in the lore that traces the origins of Rome and its early monarchy.

    According to Roman legends, the twins, Romulus and Remus, were born in Alba Longa, southeast of Rome's eventual location.

    They were from noble lineage. Their mother, a princess and daughter to the King of Numitor of Alba Longa, was usurped by his sibling, Amulius. Fearing a prophecy that the twins would end his reign, Amulius ordered their demise.

    Fate had other plans, and the Tiber River's banks, where they were abandoned, became the place of their survival. They were saved by Tiberinus, the river deity, nurtured and breastfed by a she-wolf in the sanctuary of the Lupercal cave, located at the base of Palatine Hill.

    They were then raised by the shepherd Faustulis; the twins flourished as shepherds and remained oblivious to their regal heritage.

    As adults, they were caught up in the conflict between Numitor and Amulius, which led to Remus's capture. The unfolding events led them to suspect their royalties.

    Romulus's daring rescue of Remus from captivity was the catalyst for their alliance with Numitor, resulting in his rise to the throne of Alba.

    Not content with restoring a kingdom, the twins established their own city. Yet, disagreement over which of the seven hills to form it on resulted in Remus's untimely death. They had even tried to consult with the gods over the location. Remus's death was shrouded in mystery and speculation.

    After his brother's death, Romulus became the first king of Rome. If it had been the other way around, it's possible Rome would be called “Reme” today. Furthermore, April 21st, 753 BC, became the founding date of Rome and is considered year zero in the Roman calendar.

  • Numa Pompilius

    Numa Pompilius, a native of the Sabine region, was Rome's second king after Romulus. His rise to power hinted at Rome's expanding influence. Numa's reign, from 715 to 673 BC, marked a time of great innovation for Rome.

    He introduced the Roman calendar, established the Vestal Virgins, and created the role of Pontifex Maximus, a critical religious position.

    His most notable change was adding January and February to the calendar, turning the previously ignored 51 days into recognized months, and aligning the year with the lunar cycle at 355 days.

    Plutarch tells of Numa's life as one of discipline, devoid of luxury, even though he was born on the day Rome was founded.

    Initially reluctant to rule, Numa accepted the kingship after a divine nod from Jupiter, earning him immense respect from his people. His reign brought a deeper spiritual connection, encouraging Romans to honor their gods with rituals and sacrifices devoutly.

    In a bold move, Numa dismissed his predecessor's 300-strong guard, signaling a shift from war to peace in Rome's leadership.

  • Cloelia's River Crossing

    In the wake of a fierce conflict in 508 BC, a tale emerged from the depths of Rome about a young woman named Cloelia. She was abducted by the Etruscan ruler Lars Prosena and became an unexpected symbol of resilience.

    Cloelia escaped her captors and led her fellow captives to freedom across the Tiber River. Her courageous act moved the Etruscan king, who offered her a boon: the release of half the prisoners.

    From the prisoners, she chose the most capable warriors, which ensured Rome's fight for sovereignty would endure. Today, her legacy is immortalized in stone within the Roman Forum, a testament to her bravery on the Via Sacra.
